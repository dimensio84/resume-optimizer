How to Write a Cover Letter That Actually Gets Read

Published March 18, 2026  ·  8 min read  ·  ResumeChangers Team

Most cover letters are a waste of everyone's time. They restate the resume, start with "I am writing to express my interest in the position," and end with "I look forward to hearing from you." Recruiters have read thousands of them. They skim — or skip — the generic ones entirely.

But a genuinely good cover letter can get you an interview even when you're not the most qualified candidate on paper. This guide covers exactly how to write one.

Why Cover Letters Still Matter

The debate about whether cover letters matter has a nuanced answer: it depends on who's reading your application. For large companies using ATS, your cover letter may not even be read until you've already passed automated screening. For smaller companies and competitive senior roles, it can be the deciding factor.

Where cover letters always matter: when the job posting specifically requests one. Submitting a generic or absent cover letter for a role that asks for one is an immediate red flag.

The bottom line: a bad cover letter can hurt you. A good one can be the difference between getting the call and not. Writing a good one is worth the 20 minutes.

The Structure That Works

The best cover letters follow a simple four-paragraph structure:

  1. The hook — one specific, compelling sentence that earns the reader's attention
  2. Why you're the right fit — your most relevant experience tied directly to this role's biggest need
  3. Why this company — a specific reason you want this job (not just a job)
  4. The close — a confident, brief call to action

That's it. No more than one page, 250–350 words.

The Opening: Don't Start With "I"

The opening line is the most important part of your cover letter. If it doesn't hook the reader, nothing else matters. Here's the single most common mistake:

Weak opener
"I am writing to apply for the Senior Product Manager position at Acme Corp, which I found on LinkedIn."
Strong opener
"In my last role, I led a product team that shipped a feature used by 2M users in its first month — I'd like to bring that same focus to Acme Corp's growth platform."

The strong opener leads with a result, not a formality. It immediately signals: this person has done something interesting and knows how to communicate it.

Other strong opening approaches:

  • A specific result or metric from your experience: "I've reduced customer churn by 30% at two different SaaS companies…"
  • A shared observation about the company's problem or opportunity: "When Acme announced its expansion into the enterprise segment last month, I noticed the same gap in your onboarding flow that I spent two years solving at [Company X]."
  • A connection or mutual contact: "[Name] suggested I reach out after describing my work in supply chain optimization."

The Body: Connect Your Experience to Their Problem

Paragraph two should not summarize your resume. It should answer one question: why are you the right person for this specific role's biggest challenge?

Before you write this section, read the job description carefully and identify the one or two things the employer cares most about. Usually it's explicit — the job title and top bullet points in the responsibilities section tell you everything. Then write 2-4 sentences connecting your most relevant experience to those priorities.

Example — Body Paragraph

"Your listing mentions scaling the data engineering team from 3 to 15 engineers while maintaining delivery velocity. At DataFlow Inc., I managed exactly that transition — growing from a 4-person team to 12 over 18 months while shipping our core pipeline infrastructure on schedule. I built the hiring process, onboarding program, and sprint cadence that let us do it without burning anyone out."

Notice how this is specific, tied to the job's stated need, and quantified. It's not generic. A recruiter reading this knows immediately: this person has done the thing we're asking for.

Paragraph Three: Why This Company

This paragraph separates genuine applicants from mass-appliers. Show that you've done 10 minutes of research and have a real reason for wanting this specific job — not just any job.

Good "why this company" angles:

  • A specific product, feature, or initiative you admire (and why)
  • The company's position in a market you care deeply about
  • Something a founder or executive said publicly that resonated with you
  • A problem the company is solving that you have personal experience with
Generic
"I've always admired Acme Corp's reputation as an industry leader and I believe my skills would be a great addition to your team."
Specific
"I've been following Acme's work in predictive analytics since your 2024 whitepaper on reducing hospital readmissions — it's the same problem I've been trying to solve from the provider side."

The Close: Be Direct

End simply and confidently. Don't grovel ("I would be honored to have the opportunity…"). Don't hedge ("If you think I might be a fit…"). Just close.

Strong close

"I'd welcome the chance to talk through how my background fits what you're building. Happy to make time this week."

Cover Letter Mistakes That Kill Your Chances

  • Restating your resume — recruiters already have it. Use the cover letter to add context, not repetition.
  • Addressing it to "Dear Hiring Manager" — spend 2 minutes finding the actual name on LinkedIn. It signals initiative.
  • Explaining why you want to leave your current job — keep the focus on what you're moving toward, not what you're running from.
  • Using filler phrases — "team player," "passionate," "hardworking," "results-oriented." These are noise. Replace with proof.
  • Typos or the wrong company name — this happens more than you'd think when mass-applying. Always double-check.

The one-sentence test: After writing your cover letter, ask yourself: "Could this exact letter have been written by any other applicant for this role?" If yes, rewrite it. The goal is a letter that could only have been written by you, for this specific job.

Do Cover Letters Need to Pass ATS?

Some ATS systems do parse cover letters for keywords, but the priority is your resume. That said, it never hurts to include the job title and 2–3 keywords from the job description naturally in your cover letter. This is easy to do when you're already writing specifically about the role.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do hiring managers actually read cover letters?

It depends on the company and role. Studies show about 26% of hiring managers read cover letters before deciding whether to interview a candidate, and 56% say a strong cover letter can push a borderline candidate into the interview pile. For competitive roles and smaller companies, they carry more weight.

How long should a cover letter be?

Three to four short paragraphs — no more than one page. Aim for 250–350 words. Recruiters skim; a long cover letter signals you don't respect their time.

Should I write a new cover letter for every job?

Yes, for any role you seriously want. A generic cover letter is worse than none — it signals low effort. At minimum, customize the opening paragraph and the specific skills you highlight to match each job description.

What should I never include in a cover letter?

Avoid: repeating your resume line-by-line, starting with "I am writing to apply for…", salary expectations unless requested, anything negative about past employers, and personal information that isn't relevant to the role.
