What Is an ATS and How Does It Work?

An Applicant Tracking System is software used by employers to manage job applications. When you apply online, your resume is parsed by the ATS before anyone reads it. The system extracts information — your name, job titles, skills, education — and scores your resume based on how well it matches the job description.

The ATS is essentially looking for a keyword match. It compares the words in your resume against the words in the job description. The closer the match, the higher your score. If your score falls below a threshold set by the employer, your resume is automatically rejected.

Why Good Candidates Get Filtered Out

The ATS doesn't understand context or nuance. It doesn't know that "grew revenue" and "drove sales growth" mean similar things. It doesn't appreciate that your experience is highly relevant even if you used different terminology. It just scans for exact or near-exact keyword matches.

This means you could be the most qualified candidate for a role and still get filtered out because:

  • You used "supervised" instead of "managed"
  • You wrote "MS Office" instead of "Microsoft Office"
  • You didn't include the specific software mentioned in the job posting
  • Your resume has formatting (tables, columns, headers) that the ATS can't parse correctly

How to Optimize Your Resume for ATS

1. Mirror the job description language

Read the job description carefully and note the specific words and phrases used for skills, tools, and responsibilities. Use those exact words in your resume. If the job says "project management" don't write "project coordination" — use their language.

2. Use a clean, simple format

Avoid tables, columns, text boxes, headers and footers, graphics, and fancy fonts. These confuse ATS parsers and your information can get scrambled or lost entirely. Stick to a single-column layout with standard section headings like "Experience," "Education," and "Skills."

3. Include a dedicated Skills section

ATS systems specifically look for skills sections. List your relevant hard skills, tools, technologies, and certifications clearly. Don't bury them inside job descriptions only — have a standalone section.

4. Spell out acronyms

Write both the acronym and the full term. For example: "Search Engine Optimization (SEO)" or "Customer Relationship Management (CRM)." Different ATS systems search differently — covering both bases ensures you're found.

5. Customize for every application

A generic resume will have a low ATS score for most jobs. The highest-performing resumes are tailored specifically to each job description. This is time-consuming to do manually, which is why tools like ResumeChangers exist.

Key insight: ATS optimization isn't about tricking the system — it's about communicating your experience using the same language the employer uses. A well-optimized resume is also a clearer, more relevant resume for human readers.

Common ATS Mistakes to Avoid

  • Using images or logos — ATS can't read them
  • Putting contact info in a header — many parsers skip headers
  • Using creative section titles like "Where I've Been" instead of "Experience"
  • Submitting a PDF when Word is requested — follow the employer's instructions
  • Keyword stuffing in white text — modern ATS and recruiters both catch this
