Best Resume Format for 2026: What Actually Works

Published April 25, 2026  ·  7 min read  ·  ResumeChangers Team

Ask ten resume coaches what the "best" format is and you'll get ten different answers — usually dependent on whatever template they're selling. The actual answer is more practical: the best resume format is the one that passes ATS parsing and gets read by a human in 7 seconds.

In 2026, there are three main resume formats. Two are viable. One is a trap. Here's what you need to know.

The Three Resume Formats

1. Reverse-Chronological

Best for most people

Lists your work experience in reverse order — most recent job first. This is the standard resume format and what virtually every recruiter expects to see.

  • ATS compatibility: Excellent — parsers are built for this format
  • Recruiter preference: Very high — immediately shows career progression
  • Best for: Anyone with a consistent work history in a related field

2. Hybrid (Combination)

Good for specific situations

Opens with a strong skills summary section, then follows with reverse-chronological work history. Lets you lead with relevant capabilities while still showing a credible employment timeline.

  • ATS compatibility: Good — as long as you use a single-column layout
  • Recruiter preference: Moderate — works when the skills section is substantive
  • Best for: Career changers, people with highly transferable skills, senior candidates with diverse backgrounds

3. Functional (Skills-Based)

Avoid in most cases

Groups experience by skill category rather than by employer. Designed to downplay gaps or non-linear career history — but recruiters know that, and it raises red flags.

  • ATS compatibility: Poor — many parsers can't extract employment dates correctly
  • Recruiter preference: Low — often viewed with suspicion
  • Best for: Almost no one in a standard corporate job search

The bottom line: Use reverse-chronological for 90% of job searches. If you're making a career pivot or have a highly varied background, a hybrid format is a legitimate option. Avoid functional resumes — the perception problem outweighs any benefit they provide.

What "Format" Actually Means for ATS

When most people talk about resume format, they mean the structural layout — which section comes first. But for ATS compatibility, visual formatting matters just as much as structural formatting. The way your document is built can make or break how an ATS parses your information.

Single-column vs. multi-column layouts

Multi-column resumes look polished. Canva and many Word templates default to them. But ATS systems read left-to-right, line-by-line, which means a two-column layout causes the parser to scramble your content — your skills from the right column get mixed into your job titles from the left, or your contact information disappears entirely.

Stick to a single-column layout for any resume submitted digitally. Save multi-column designs for physical copies at career fairs, where an ATS won't touch them.

Avoid these formatting elements

  • Tables — ATS parsers can't reliably extract content from table cells
  • Text boxes — often skipped entirely by parsers
  • Headers and footers — contact info placed here may not be parsed
  • Graphics and icons — completely unreadable by ATS
  • Embedded images — including photos of yourself
  • Non-standard fonts — some substitute as unreadable characters
  • Horizontal lines between sections — can disrupt parsing in some systems

Should You Use a Template?

Templates are fine — with caveats. The problem is that most popular resume templates (especially from Canva, Adobe, and many Word themes) were designed by graphic designers optimizing for visual appeal, not ATS compatibility. They look great as PDFs and fail completely when parsed by software.

If you use a template, check for these red flags:

  • Two or more columns
  • A sidebar with contact info or skills
  • Progress bars or visual skill ratings
  • Color blocks or shaded backgrounds behind text
  • Custom icons for sections

All of these will hurt your ATS score. A plain, well-organized single-column Word document with consistent formatting consistently outperforms visually elaborate templates in automated screening.

The Right Section Order in 2026

For a reverse-chronological resume, the section order that works best for both ATS and human readers:

  1. Contact information
  2. Professional summary (2–4 sentences)
  3. Work experience (reverse chronological)
  4. Skills
  5. Education
  6. Optional sections (certifications, projects, etc.)

Move education above work experience only if you're a recent graduate with limited work history. Otherwise, experience comes first — recruiters want to see what you've done before they care where you went to school.

PDF vs. Word: Which to Submit?

The answer is almost always: follow the instructions in the job posting. If the employer specifies a format, use it exactly.

When no format is specified:

  • Word (.docx) is generally safer for ATS parsing — the format was designed to be machine-readable
  • PDF preserves your visual formatting perfectly but some older ATS systems struggle with PDF parsing

If you're applying through a large company's online portal, .docx is the lower-risk choice. If you're emailing your resume directly to a hiring manager (who will read it themselves), PDF is fine.

Formatting Details That Matter

Fonts

Safe choices: Calibri, Arial, Georgia, Garamond, Cambria. Use 10–12pt for body text, 14–18pt for your name, 11–13pt bold for section headings. Avoid decorative, script, condensed, or unusual fonts.

Margins

0.5 to 1 inch on all sides. Going narrower than 0.5" makes the document feel cramped; going wider wastes space. 0.75" is a solid default.

Line spacing

Single spacing (1.0–1.15) within sections; a blank line or small spacing increase between sections. Consistent spacing throughout signals attention to detail.

Bullet points

Standard round bullets (•) are safest. Some ATS systems misread special bullets, em dashes, or arrows as text characters. Keep it simple.

Quick Checklist: Is Your Resume Format Right for 2026?

  • Single-column layout
  • Reverse-chronological experience (most recent first)
  • Contact info in the document body (not a header/footer)
  • No tables, text boxes, or graphics
  • Standard section headings (Experience, Education, Skills)
  • Clean, standard font at 10–12pt body size
  • Consistent date formatting throughout
  • Submitted as .docx when ATS processing is likely

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the best resume format in 2026?

The reverse-chronological format is the best choice for the vast majority of job seekers. It's what recruiters expect, it's fully compatible with ATS systems, and it lets your most recent and relevant experience lead. Avoid the functional format — most recruiters distrust it and many ATS systems can't parse it correctly.

Is a two-column resume format bad for ATS?

Yes — two-column and multi-column resume formats frequently confuse ATS parsers. The software reads left-to-right, top-to-bottom, which means content in side columns often gets scrambled or skipped entirely. Stick to a single-column layout for any resume submitted through an online application.

Should I use a resume template from Word or Canva?

Use Word templates with caution and avoid Canva for resumes submitted through online applications. Canva resumes are image-based and completely unreadable by ATS systems. Many Word templates also use tables and text boxes that break ATS parsing. Choose a template explicitly labeled as ATS-compatible, or build a simple single-column document from scratch.

What font and font size should I use on my resume?

Use a clean, standard font: Calibri, Arial, Garamond, Georgia, or Cambria are all safe choices. Body text should be 10–12pt. Your name at the top can be 14–18pt. Section headings work well at 11–13pt bold. Avoid decorative, script, or condensed fonts.
