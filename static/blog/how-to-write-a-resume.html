How to Write a Resume in 2026 (Step-by-Step Guide)

Published April 21, 2026  ·  9 min read  ·  ResumeChangers Team

Before You Start: The One Rule That Changes Everything

A resume is not a history of everything you've done. It's a marketing document targeted at a specific job. Every word should answer one question: "Why should we hire this person for this role?"

This means a great resume is almost never the same document twice. The foundation stays consistent, but the language, the emphasis, and the skills you highlight should shift for each application. Keep that in mind as you write.

Step 1: Choose the Right Format

Most people should use a reverse-chronological format — your most recent job first, working backwards. This is what recruiters expect and what ATS systems parse most reliably.

A functional or skills-based format (which leads with skills instead of jobs) is tempting if you have gaps or are changing careers, but most recruiters distrust it. It obscures your timeline and often flags as suspicious. Stick to chronological unless you have a compelling reason not to.

For a deeper look at format tradeoffs, see our guide on Chronological vs. Functional Resume: Which Format Should You Use?

Step 2: Set Up Your Contact Section

Place your contact details at the very top, in the document body (not a header or text box, which ATS parsers often skip). Include:

  • Full name — larger font, bold
  • City and state — no full street address needed
  • Phone number
  • Professional email address — firstname.lastname@gmail.com, not a nickname
  • LinkedIn URL — shortened (linkedin.com/in/yourname)
  • Portfolio or GitHub — if relevant to your field

Common mistake: Using an unprofessional email address like "partyguy1987@" or "xoxocutie@" will get you screened out before anyone reads a single bullet point. Create a clean gmail address if yours is embarrassing.

Step 3: Write a Professional Summary

The professional summary sits below your contact info and is 2–4 sentences that frame who you are and what you bring to this specific role. It's the first thing a recruiter reads after your name — make it count.

A strong summary answers three questions: Who are you professionally? What's your biggest value proposition? What are you looking for?

Before (weak)

"Hardworking and motivated professional seeking a challenging position where I can utilize my skills and grow within a dynamic organization."

After (strong)

"Results-driven marketing manager with 7 years of experience growing B2B SaaS brands. Led campaigns generating $4M in pipeline at two Series B startups. Looking to bring full-funnel expertise to a growth-stage tech company."

Notice the difference: the strong version is specific, quantified, and targeted. It tells the reader exactly what they're getting. For a full breakdown, see our guide on Resume Summary vs. Objective: Which One to Use.

Step 4: Write Your Work Experience

This is the heart of your resume. For each role, include the company name, your title, dates (Month Year – Month Year), and 3–6 bullet points describing your impact.

How to write strong bullet points

The formula: Action verb + What you did + Result or scale. Lead every bullet with a strong past-tense action verb. Quantify wherever you can — numbers, percentages, dollar amounts, and timeframes make bullets credible and memorable.

Before (weak)

"Responsible for managing the social media accounts and helping grow the company's online presence."

After (strong)

"Grew Instagram following from 4,200 to 31,000 in 12 months through a content series that averaged 8% engagement rate — 4× the industry benchmark."

Not sure how to add numbers to a role that doesn't feel measurable? Read our guide on How to Quantify Your Achievements on a Resume.

How many bullets per job?

  • Most recent / most relevant role: 5–6 bullets
  • Mid-tenure roles: 3–4 bullets
  • Older or less relevant roles: 1–2 bullets, or title/company/dates only

What about gaps?

Employment gaps are more common than ever and less stigmatized than they used to be. Address them briefly and confidently — in your summary if substantial, or in a cover letter. Don't try to hide a gap by listing only years (2022–2024) without months — recruiters notice. See our guide on How to Explain Employment Gaps on Your Resume.

Step 5: Add Your Education Section

For most working professionals, education sits below work experience and is brief: degree, major, institution, and graduation year. Skip GPA unless you graduated recently and it was above 3.5.

If you're a recent graduate with limited work experience, move education above your experience and expand it — include relevant coursework, GPA (if strong), academic projects, and honors.

Step 6: Build a Skills Section

A dedicated skills section serves two purposes: it makes your qualifications scannable for human readers, and it gives ATS systems a clear place to find your keywords. Include:

  • Hard skills — software, tools, technologies, methodologies (e.g., Python, Salesforce, Google Analytics, Agile)
  • Certifications and credentials — PMP, AWS, CPA, etc.
  • Languages — if relevant

Skip generic soft skills like "team player" and "good communicator" — every candidate claims these and they mean nothing in a skills list. Demonstrate them in your bullet points instead.

ATS tip: Mirror the exact language from the job description in your skills section. If the posting says "customer relationship management (CRM)" don't just write "Salesforce" — write both. ATS systems search for different variations. See our guide on How to Find the Right Keywords for Your Resume.

Step 7: Add Optional Sections (Only If They Add Value)

Depending on your background and target role, consider including:

  • Projects — especially valuable for developers, designers, and recent grads
  • Certifications — if not in skills, list with issuing organization and date
  • Volunteer work — if relevant to the role or fills a gap meaningfully
  • Publications or speaking — for academic and thought-leadership roles
  • Awards and honors — sparingly, only if genuinely notable

Every section you add takes up space and recruiter attention. Include optional sections only when they strengthen your case for the specific role.

Step 8: Format for Readability and ATS Compatibility

Your resume needs to pass an ATS parser and hold up to a 7-second human scan. Both goals point to the same formatting principles:

  • Single-column layout — multi-column formats often break ATS parsing
  • Standard section headings — "Experience" not "Where I've Been," "Education" not "My Learning Journey"
  • Clean fonts — Calibri, Arial, Georgia, or similar. 10–12pt for body, 14–16pt for your name
  • Consistent spacing — uniform margins (0.5–1 inch), consistent date formatting
  • No tables, text boxes, columns, or graphics — these confuse ATS parsers
  • No headers or footers for contact info — many ATS systems skip them

Step 9: Tailor Before You Send

A polished generic resume will still underperform a tailored one. Before every application, review the job description and:

  1. Add any keywords from the job posting you're missing
  2. Reorder your bullet points so the most relevant ones are first
  3. Adjust your summary to reflect the specific role and company
  4. Check that your skills section matches the tools listed in the posting

Common Resume Mistakes to Avoid

  • Writing in first person — "I managed a team of 5" should be "Managed a team of 5"
  • Using a generic objective statement — replace it with a targeted summary
  • Listing duties instead of achievements — "Responsible for X" vs. "Achieved Y by doing X"
  • Including references or "References available upon request" — wastes space, everyone knows
  • Sending a PDF when Word is requested — always follow the employer's instructions
  • Using the same resume for every job — tailor it every time

Frequently Asked Questions

What sections should every resume include?

Every resume should include: contact information, a professional summary, work experience, education, and a skills section. Depending on your background you may also add certifications, projects, or volunteer work. Keep optional sections only if they add relevant value.

How long should a resume be?

One page for candidates with under 10 years of experience. Two pages are acceptable for senior professionals with extensive relevant history. The rule is: every line should earn its place. Padding a resume to fill space hurts more than it helps.

Should I include a photo on my resume?

In the United States, Canada, and the UK, do not include a photo. Photos can introduce unconscious bias and are not expected. In some European and Asian countries, photos are standard — follow the conventions for the country where you are applying.

How far back should my work experience go?

Generally 10–15 years is sufficient. Older roles can be listed without bullet points as a brief line item if they add context. Focus on recency and relevance over completeness.
